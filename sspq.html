When can you reset CB’s?

Deliberate shutdown of serviceable equipment by opening of CBs is not permitted, except as required by checklist procedures.

Flight crew reset of tripped fuel-pump and fuel-pump-control CBs is prohibited (both on the ground and during flight).

Reset of any other tripped CB is not recommended unless, in the judgement of the Captain, the situation resulting from the CB trip has a significant adverse effect on safety. These other tripped CBs may be reset once, after a short cooling period (approx 2 minutes). A ground reset of a tripped CB by the flight crew should only be accomplished after Engineering has determined that it is safe to reset the CB.

Flight crew must raise a Technical Log/eLog entry any time they reset a CB.

What are the critical phases of flight?

The critical phases of flight are defined as:

  • all operations from off blocks to 10,000 ft
  • when within 1,000 ft of any level off or transition
  • approaching/commencing descent
  • from 10,000 ft to on blocks

No flight crew member shall perform any duty during a critical phase of flight except those duties required for the safe operation of the aircraft.

Paperwork, PAs and non-operational conversation should be kept to a minimum.

SOP 2.1.2 Critical Phases of Flight

When must you wear safety harness?

Flight crew are required to have their seat belts fastened when occupying the appropriate flight-deck seat between engine start and shutdown, or when the aircraft is under tow.

It is recommended that the full safety harness is worn at all times during flight.

Full safety harness must be worn:

  • during take-off and from at least five minutes prior to landing (transition on descent is a good indicator)
  • when the aircraft is flying at less than 1,000 ft AGL
  • during an instrument approach for landing
  • during turbulent conditions, or when turbulence is expected
  • in an emergency
  • when the aircraft is taxiing (except any operating crew members occurpying an observer's seat)
  • when either pilot seat is unoccupied

Jet Upset definition?

An aircraft upset is an undesired aircraft state characterised by unintentional divergences from parameters normally experienced during operations. An aircraft upset may involve pitch and/or bank angle divergences and may lead to inappropriate airspeeds for the conditions. An upset condition exists any time an aircraft diverges from what the flight crew is intending to do. Deviations from the desired aircraft state will become larger until the flight crew takes action to stop the divergence. Return to the desired aircraft state can be achieved through natural aircraft reaction to accelerations, auto-flight system response or flight crew intervention.

What is Emergency talk?

Flight crew who deem a Captain’s actions to be extremely unsafe, and can foresee the potential for an incident or accident, must be able to influence the outcome. Emergency talk is the formal procedure used to achieve this. Ultimately, the use of a key phrase obliges the Captain to reconsider the course of action.

  • Enquiry – What is your plan from here? How will we manage our height/speed from here?
  • Advocacy – I think we should ask for extra track miles. Max airspeed below 5000ft is 250kts.
  • Assertion – Turn on to heading 050. Reduce speed now.
  • “Captain, you must listen” – Submit OSR if phrase has been used

What is the difference between MRC and LRC?

MRC is the most distance for the least amount of fuel burnt. With MRC you do not care how long it takes to fly the most distance. This means operating the aircraft at the best lift to drag ratio. As weight reduces during flight less lift will be required to balance the reducing weight component. MRC therefore reduces as the aircraft gets lighter.

LRC burns 1% extra fuel but this gives you a 5% increase in airspeed.

What is the definition of contamination?

A runway is considered to be contaminated when more than 25% of the runway surface area (whether in isolated areas or not) within the required length and width being used is covered by any of the following:

  • Standing water or slush more than 3mm in depth
  • Wet snow more than 3mm in depth
  • Dry snow more than 3mm in depth
  • Any depth of compacted snow or ice, including wet ice

When can you carry out a flex take-off?

Reduced thrust takeoffs may be made provided:

  1. Reduced thrust flex temperature data is shown on TODC output.
  2. The ambient temperature is lower than the flex temperature for the actual brake release weight.
  3. No significant windshear is suspected to exist in the takeoff path.
  4. There are no MEL items which preclude reduced thrust takeoffs.

A reduced thrust takeoff is only permitted when using TODC output produced for the runway is use. Reduced thrust takeoff is NOT permitted when:

  • Taking off towards WIP in which men or machinery are on the runway or strip, or
  • Using General Data (TODC inoperative), or
  • Using airport GEND (in PER-TOF-TOD-14-10) in the TODC, or
  • Using any paper individual runway table

TOGA thrust must be used in the above cases.

Note: If in any doubt about the suitability of the conditions use TOGA thrust.

SOP 2.1.8

Engine failure past the EDTO entry which type of airport must you continue to?

In-flight Contingency. In the event of an in-flight shutdown of an engine, the pilot should fly to and land, in the first instance, at the nearest EDTO alternate aerodrome (in terms of flying time) at which a safe landing can be made.

Note: This is NOT RESTRICTED to the flight planned EDTO en-route alternates, and any EDTO alternate aerodromes, departure and destination airports may be considered.

In the event of a failure of a system or system element, for which no suitable redundancy exists, the pilot should fly to and land at, the nearest EDTO alternate aerodrome. In this context, primary systems are: electrical, hydraulics and pneumatics. It may be reasonable to continue flight to a more distant aerodrome using the APU to provide backup services. However, if APU operation is required at any time prior to a limiting ETP, sufficient fuel must be available to ensure that the remaining in-flight requirements are not compromised.

In all cases, the PIC has the authority to act in the most prudent manner when assessing the situation at the time, considering all factors of:

  • Aircraft serviceability
  • Aerodrome suitability
  • Weather conditions

In-flight, type specific QRH/FCOM will prescribe those failures, or combination of failures, for which a diversion is required.

RG 1 OPS.24/5 Extended Diversion Time Operations (EDTO) – Considerations

  • Electrical Generation and Hydraulic Systems require diversion if APU does not start or the APU GEN fails.
  • Fuel System failures which result in a lower level being flown and gravity feeding making some fuel unusable.
  • Issues affecting the blue hydraulic system may also require a divert.

FCOM PRO-SPO-EDTO-Diversion Decision Making

What requires a diversion?

Non-normal procedures, when appropriate, contain information that crews need to consider when making decisions concerning the continuation or termination of the flight. While such decisions can only be made by the PIC after consideration of all relevant factors, there are some conditions that always require a landing at the nearest (in time) suitable airport at which a safe landing can be made. These factors include, but are not limited to:

  • Failures leading to LAND ASAP on the ECAM/QRH
  • Engine fire or failure
  • Wheel-well fire
  • Fuselage fire
  • Cargo fire
  • Cabin smoke or fire that cannot be immediately and positively determined to be eliminated or extinguished
  • One hydraulic system remaining
  • -On AC power source remaining
  • Any other condition determined by the crew to present a significant adverse effect on the safety if the flight is continued
  • Where regulations require an early landing

In each case, it is the responsibility of the PIC to assess the conditions and execute sound judgement to determine the safest course of action.

SOP 4.1.2 Diversion Decision

In-flight you are predicting to be below DDP fuel requirements. What are the conditions for continuing to destination & do you need to maintain critical ETP fuel?

Should it become apparent at any time prior to the DDP that the MIN DDP FUEL will not be available at the DDP, the PIC may elect to continue to the destination based on assessment of the following:

  • - Actual and forecast destination and destination alternate weather for the expected ETA. (suitable closer alternates can be considered)
  • - Fuel predictions based upon updated winds, updated flight plan and expected approach at destination
  • - The ability to divert to the destination alternate from top of descent or at a lower level (thereby gaining fuel, see fuel savings by not descending)
  • - Maintain awareness of critical fuel including any mitigation available to reduce fuel requirements
  • - Preservation of minimum fixed fuel reserve

If, after considering all available information, the PIC is NOT satisfied that a safe landing with the required reserves is assured, then the aircraft should be diverted to the en-route refuelling airfield.

What is the Approach Integrity Check (AIC)?

Approach Integrity Check consists of the following:

  • The procedure must be in the database
  • Lateral track within +/- 1˚
  • Angle must be correct within +/- 0.07˚
  • Vertical FMGS heights must be at or above the charted crossing altitudes. Also the lowest coded altitude after the FAF must be equal or lower than DA/MDA
  • Monitor Raw data when available

RG1 NAV.APP.3

If the AIC fails then you cannot use final app for guidance.

What is are Instrument approach hierarchy?

When an instrument approach is required, the crew should apply the following order of preference to the category appropriate to the conditions:

  1. GLS
  2. RNAV (APV) or ILS (APV = Approach with lateral and vertical guidance, but not a precision approach)
  3. RNAV (NPA) (NPA = non-precision approach which utilises lateral but not vertical guidance)
  4. NPAs in the following order:
    1. LOC/DME
    2. LOC or LDA
    3. VOR/DME
    4. VOR
    5. NDB/DME
    6. NDB

RNAV (RNP) AR approaches must be validated by the Air New Zealand navigation services validation process. (ie: not by pilots carrying out an AIC)

In an emergency evacuation after an unprepared landing, what can you expect the FA to be prepared to do?

Crew Co-ordination General matrix states:

  • Quick/Prepared Emergency Landing – cabin crew should be prepared to act as required after landing. This could include an evacuation.

CSM 4.5.1 Crew Co-ordination General

Flight attendant initiated evacuation (if cabin crew have identified the lives of the passengers/crew are in immediate danger).

  • All effort should be made in the first instance to contact the flight deck via interphone. This will ensure the engines are shut down and the aircraft is stopped
    • In extreme circumstances and if contact with the flight deck is not possible, be prepared to act without instruction from the flight crew.

CSM 4.14.1 Evacuation Objective

Actions in the event of cabin not ready on landing?

If cabin clearance is not received by flight crew at the appropriate time before landing, they will make a PA announcement “Flight attendants must now be seated for landing”.
