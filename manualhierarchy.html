Manual Hierarchy

  • back

The hierarchy of manuals can be found in the Airline Management System Manual. This lists all the documents refered to as part of the Air Operator Exposition, part 119, that the company holds. Key points from the Airline Management System Manual are:

  • Manuals show airline structure, the safety management system, operational policy and procedures which define means and methods to comply with NZ CAA Rules, IATA and company standards.
  • Exemptions to rules may be granted by the NZ CAA.
  • Exposition documents must be approved by the NZ CAA before any changes are incorporated or implemented.

Cabin Safety Manual

The Cabin Safety Manual provides information regarding what we will do in an abnormal situation from a crew and passenger handling point of view. The CSM includes flight attendant standard operating procedures, emergency equipment and emergency procedures. This includes emergency landing and evacuation procedures. There is an aircraft specific section which covers regional or domestic aircraft but not NEO cabin differences. Lastly, there are sections for medical issues, survival, dangerous goods and onboard security.

RG1

Route Guide 1 holds company policy regarding different equipment and operations.

  • General procedures section: information regarding items such as handling of unruly passengers and and reporting bird hazards etc.
  • Operation section: provides some of the relevant IFR rules we must comply with...but not everything, see jeppensen for more information, RVSM requirements.
  • Communications and FANS sections: show how to work different on board comms equipment and a list of different department numbers to call.
  • Flight planning section: includes the fuel policy and flow charts to follow for deciding if an alternate is required.
  • Quick reference tables: provided to check company minima regarding nomination of alternates and EDTO requiments.
  • Navigation section: information about airspace we are operating in and equipment requirements.
  • Fleet section: holds A320/321 specific information.

Standard Operating Procedures

SOPs are the company approved procedures for use by all fleets. SOPs enhance safety and efficiency by providing policies, guidance and a model of operation that crews are expected to follow.

QRH

The QRH outlines the WHO does the task. The QRH has abnormal and emergency procedures, normal procedures, in-flight performance and operational data. Any OEB actions required by flight crew. Company SOPs for abnormal situations from a change of runway through to precautionary and emergency landings, emergency evacuations and incident follow up. There is also sections on Low Minima and Cold Weather Operations.

FCOM

The FCOM provides information about procedures and systems, the WHAT. The FCOM is the a support document for the flight crew. The FCOM complements the Airplane Flight Manual (AFM). If the FCOM data differs from the AFM, the AFM remains the main reference. As a supplement to the FCOM, the FCTM may provide additional information that the flight crew should read in conjunction with the FCOM. (Company AFM are located in each individual aircraft and electronic access is not provided to pilots.) Ref: FCOM Purpose GEN P 28-29/60.

OEB

Operations Engineering Bullentins (OEBs) are issued when it is necessary to rapidly trasmit technical and procedural information. This may later be included to the FCOM permanently or have the issue resolved and OEB deleted. Ref: FCOM Revisions GEN P 37/60.

Supp 1

Important operational information that needs to made available immediately to all affected flight crew. Includes; aircraft restrictions, up dated systems information and new aerodrome procedures. Each item is meant to be incorporated into a relevant manual or deleted if expired.

FCTM

FCTM provides additional information to the FCOM procedures, the WHY to do and HOW to do. The FCTM provides complementary information to the FCOM. Wherever FCTM information differs from FCOM, the FCOM remains the reference. Ref: FCTM GI P 1/22.

MEL/DDG/CDL

Adapted from the Airbus master MMEL for each companies specific operations. The MEL/DDG/CDL is allowed to be more restrictive than the MMEL and takes into account company policy. MEL AP-03 P 1/4. Prior to dispatch the MEL/DDG/CDL must be consulted for any failures. An MEL strategy table is provided to guide pilots as to actions after dispatch and prior to take-off should an issue occur. Ref: MEL MI-00-02 P 1/2. The MEL only accounts for individual failures, it is up to the pilots to display safe and sound reasoning when contemplating multiple MELs together. Ref: ????. Dispatch conditions indicate any additional conditions or limitations that must be in place to ensure an acceptable level of safety to maintained. Ref: MEL HOW P 10/24. Flight crew are responsible for checking the conditions of dispatch in the MEL are complied with, if not dispatch is not permitted. Ref: MEL HOW P 9/24.

ICAO - Dangerous Goods Red Book

The primary source of information involving dangerous goods. Includes checklists for differing dangerous goods incidents and aircraft emergency response drill tables. To be used in conjunction with Route Guide 1, General, and Cabin Safety Manual, Section 8, which outline Air New Zealand specific procedures.

Technical Bulletins

Released by Air New Zealand Flight Operations and Safety to raise the awareness of the pilot group regarding technical issues. Technical bulletins are information only.

Memo Book

Information the company wishes to distirbute which is not important enough for Supp 1. Examples include: iPad updates, vacant positions, fleet TDPD and training information.

Supp 2

Everyone's favourite manual. Lists the allowances to be collected on each duty and at each port. Like any good comrade, trust but verify.
